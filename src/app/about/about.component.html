Yet another evolutionary simulation by Scott Schafer

This is the first and simplest evolving artifical life simulation that I ever made, ported from C++/Windows (16-bit!) to modern web technologies - and of course with some new features added. This version should be very easy to hack on - just follow the instuctions on the github project page.

My goal was to create the simplest possible simulation in which inheritance, random mutation and natural selection lead to interesting adaptations. Along the way I've been continually surprised how the simulated organisms (aka 'Critters') evolve to use (or not) the instructions I've made available to them, sometimes in surprising and even apparently "inventive" ways.

Each of the simulated organisms (aka 'Critters') has a simple genome. Unlike the chemical ATGC encoding of you and me, the "genomes" in this simulation is made up of instructions which are excuted sequentially, looping from the end back to the start, and a critter is as long as its number of instructions. A critter has energy, which goes down every turn (more if they move or Move and eat), and goes up when they photosynthesize or eat.

Below you will see how a critter executes its instructions, losing and gaining energy in the process. The green squares are food, the red moving thing is the critter, and the white line at its head indicates the direction it is facing.

This is an interactive view - you can use the buttons on the lower-left to speed up / slow down / play or step through the critter's actions.

There are three ways a critter can gain energy:

  1. It can photosynthesize. The * instruction indicates that this cell should be a photosynthesis cell. A critter gains energy every turn for each exposed photosynthesis cell. A photosynthesis cell is always bright green, even if part of a moving critter.
  2. It can exececute a Move and eat instruction so that its head moves onto a Food element (dark green).
  3. It can exececute a Move and eat instruction so that its head moves onto the photosynthesis cell of another critter. This inflicts damage on the other critter and gains eenrgy for the critter doing the biting.

If an organism's energy goes to zero, it dies and becomes food. If its energy exceeds its spawn energy, it produces an offspring which may have a mutation in its instruction set - a random instruction randomly added, modified or deleted.

You can click on the simulation window and drag to move the magnifying glass and play around with the various options, such as adding obstacles or tweaking different parameters. You can also insert your own critters. Have fun!
